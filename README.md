Compound interest

The core mechanism of compound interest is the process where the interest you earn on an initial sum of money earns interest itself over subsequent periods. This creates an accelerating growth effect, contrasting with simple interest, which only calculates returns on the original principal. To mathematically calculate this growth, we use a standard formula that computes the total accumulated future value, including both the original principal and the accrued interest.

The standard mathematical formula states that the total accumulated amount equals the principal multiplied by the quantity of one plus the annual interest rate divided by the compounding frequency, with that entire quantity raised to the power of the compounding frequency multiplied by time in years. In standard notation, this is written as:

$$A = P \left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}$$

Breaking down these components logically allows us to see how each variable drives the final outcome. The principal, represented by the letter P, is the initial lump sum of money invested or borrowed before any interest begins to accumulate. The annual interest rate, represented by the lowercase r, is the nominal percentage rate expressed as a decimal; for example, a five percent rate is written as zero point zero five. The compounding frequency, represented by the letter n, dictates how many times per year the interest is calculated and added back into the principal balance, such as once for annually, four times for quarterly, or twelve times for monthly. Finally, the time, represented by the letter t, is measured as the total number of years the money is left to grow.

The mathematical mechanism works through a precise sequence of operations. First, dividing the annual interest rate by the compounding frequency gives the specific interest rate applied to each individual compounding period. Adding one to this figure creates a total growth factor for a single period. Raising this growth factor to the power of the total number of periods—which is the compounding frequency multiplied by the total years—simulates the exponential compounding effect over time. Finally, multiplying this entire exponential multiplier by the original principal reveals the final total value of the account. To find the isolated compound interest earned on its own, you simply subtract the initial principal from this final calculated amount.
